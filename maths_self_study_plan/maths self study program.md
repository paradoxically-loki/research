# Core areas to systematize, and what to read

## Real Analysis (the foundation everything else rests on)

- Understanding Analysis by Abbott — gentle, rigorous, excellent for self-study
 Principles of Mathematical Analysis by Rudin — harder, but the gold standard; use after Abbott if you want depth

## Linear Algebra (go beyond the computational)

- Linear Algebra Done Right by Axler — proof-based, beautiful, free online legally
- Matrix Analysis by Horn & Johnson — if you later need the advanced stuff (eigenvalue theory, etc.)

## Probability & Measure Theory

- Probability and Measure by Billingsley — the classic rigorous treatment; hard but worth it
- Probability: Theory and Examples by Durrett — more accessible, also very good; free PDF on his website
Start with one of these only after you're comfortable with real analysis

## Stochastic Calculus (to go deeper than you currently are)

- Brownian Motion and Stochastic Calculus by Karatzas & Shreve — the standard rigorous reference; dense
- Introduction to Stochastic Calculus with Applications by Klebaner — more accessible
- Stochastic Differential Equations by Øksendal — compact and very readable; a good middle ground

## Statistics & Inference

- All of Statistics by Wasserman — excellent breadth, great for quant finance background
- Statistical Inference by Casella & Berger — more thorough if you want depth

## Optimization (very useful in quant finance)

- Convex Optimization by Boyd & Vandenberghe — free PDF online, absolutely essential
- Numerical Optimization by Nocedal & Wright — if you want the algorithms side too

## Partial Differential Equations (needed for derivatives pricing)

- PDEs for Scientists and Engineers by Farlow — intuitive, great for self-study
- Evans PDE — if you later want the full rigorous treatment


# Strategy for self-study
- Work problems, not just chapters. Reading mathematics passively is nearly useless. Do exercises — even just 3–5 per section. This is non-negotiable.
- Use the "three-pass" method. First pass: skim for structure. Second pass: read carefully, no skipping. Third pass: close the book and reconstruct key arguments from memory.
- Don't read linearly across too many books at once. Pick one primary text per subject and go deep before moving on. Having 4 books open simultaneously usually means finishing none.
- Interleave theory with application. When studying measure-theoretic probability, simultaneously revisit a stochastic calculus result you already know — this cements both.
- Keep a "mathematical journal." Write proofs in your own words, note confusions, track which exercises stumped you. This is especially valuable in isolated self-study.
- Spaced repetition for definitions and theorems. Tools like Anki work surprisingly well for mathematical vocabulary and key results.

## Suggested sequencing given your background
Since you already have calculus, linear algebra, and some stochastic calculus:

- Abbott (Real Analysis) — builds rigor
- Axler (Linear Algebra) in parallel — proof-based thinking
- Durrett (Probability) — once analysis is solid
- Øksendal or Karatzas & Shreve — deepening your stochastic calculus
- Boyd (Convex Optimization) — can be done almost any time, fairly self-contained
- Wasserman (Statistics) — can also run in parallel relatively early


# Resources beyond books

- MIT OpenCourseWare — full lecture notes and problem sets for real analysis (18.100), probability (18.650), etc.
- Paul's Online Math Notes — surprisingly good for quick ODE/PDE review
- 3Blue1Brown (YouTube) — for rebuilding geometric intuition, especially linear algebra and calculus
- Stochastic calculus notes by Lalley or Bass — freely available rigorous lecture notes
- Math StackExchange — invaluable when you're stuck; learning to ask good questions there is itself a skill

The most important thing: consistency over intensity. An hour a day beats a 10-hour weekend session that leaves you burned out. Given you'll be working full-time, protecting even 45 minutes of focused study daily will compound enormously over a year or two.
